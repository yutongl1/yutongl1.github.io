Endangered Animals

A study of endangered aminal groups and population trend

Accountability

Where did this data come from? Who has access to it?

The dataset is extracted from the IUCN Red List , which is known as the most comprehensive, objective global approach for evaluating the conservation status of plant and animal species [3] . It records the species ID, kingdom, phylum, calss, order, family, genus, species, common names, Red List criteria, year assesses, population trend, petitioned, etc. information about species.

Everyone can get access to the dataset from the website of the IUCN Red List freely.

Completeness

Are the data complete?

As shown above, the data are incomplete. The detailed information about species are collected periodically, usually at least once every four years, which means there exist gaps in the data set. That is why some data, like the data of 2005, 2007, 2011, are unnormally low. Also, this might be caused by incompletable information collected, since the accessing process is time-consuming and costs money.

And only a small number of the world's animal species have been assessed. In addition to the many thousands of species which have not yet been assessed (i.e., are Not Evaluated), other species that are not included on The IUCN Red List are those that went extinct before 1500 AD and Least Concern species that have not yet been data based. The species groups that have been comprehensively assessed include the amphibians, birds, mammals, freshwater crabs, warm-water reef building corals, conifers and cycads. The vast majority of plants listed in the 1997 IUCN Red List of Threatened Plants have not yet been evaluated against the revised Red List Criteria and are therefore not included here. [3]

But for our study, which mainly focuses on the trend of endangered animals, the percentage of each category can make sense.

Coherence

Does it make sense relative to itself? Does it match your expectations? Are the distributions sensible? What is your evidence for this?

Looking into the original dataset, it includes all the information we need: particular categories of animals, years accessed, Red List status, and population trend. And they all make sense, and can be used for our studies as can be seen in our homepage, that is we expect for.

But the data are imbalanced, as shown in Section Completeness. There are fewer data in some years like 2006. That might be caused by less assessment, but we can get some insights from the relative percentage of different categories. Thus, we decided to analyze the percentage of each category to get over this problem.

Correctness

Is the data correct?

The original dataset was collected by the IUCN Species Programme, and the IUCN Species Survival Commission , and plays an important role in guiding conservation activities of governments, NGOs and scientific institutions [4]. Thus, our dataset should be the one which we can get as correct as possible now.
